Reliability and Interdependency

Reliability

Referring to literature...

the reliability of a system is described as its probability to deliver the expected service within a certain time period.

(Some add to the definition "under given external / environmental conditions".

Reliability analysis

  • Time-consuming
  • In detail hard for complex systems
  • In particular if not everything is self-developed
  • Standard methods
    • Failure mode and effect analysis FMEA
    • Fault-tree analysis (FTA)
    • System theoretic process analysis (STPA)

Reliability vs Risk

Instead focussing on reliability values we evaluate the risk of the system:


Risk: "An expression for the potential effect of a failure and its frequency of occurrence."

\[R = fC\]

General idea: Reducing failure risks improves reliability.


(Although a relation is not known...)

Two principal approaches for modelling interdependencies

  • Predictive: Analysis of potential events (focused)
  • Empirical: Investigation of previous events

Predictive Interdependency Modelling

  • Investigation of systems in current design
  • How an initial events propagates (different scopes possible)
  • Tracking functional changes at component/system borders

Methods of predictive modelling

  • Expert modelling
  • Mathematical simulation
  • Agent-based simulation

Challenges of interdependency modelling

  • Large Systems, with many, not completely known components are difficult to describe
  • Dynamic behaviour hard to describe (depending on environmental conditions, wear, etc.)

Reliablity and interdependencies

Interdependencies between infrastructures create paths along which failures distribute throughout the system.

Understanding this potential paths is necessary in order to evaluate the probability that a system remains operational in case of failures.

The CCP Interdependency Model

Interdependency Overview (System scope)

Analysing Interdependencies

Goal: Finding out the impacts of interdependencies on reliablity

Three steps:

  1. Select and describe initiating events
  2. Qualitative interdependency analysis
  3. Quantitative interdependency analysis

Selecting initiating event

  • Creating a list of potential hazardous events
  • Methods:
    • Preliminary Hazard analysis (expert supported)
    • Risk matrices

In the project this will be conducted for the demonstrators later.

Describing the initiating events

A detailed description of the events is required. This includes:

  • Physical location
  • Environmental conditions and constraints
  • Spatial and temporal scales
  • Technical and organisational systems
  • Operating factors
  • Physical objects that are affected immediately
  • Immediately affected (critical) system functions

CCP: Use cases are important functions

Example: Technical UCs:

  • Fast sustainable charging (UCT1)
  • Route planning by EV driver (UCT2)
  • Use of Surplus Energy for Cheap Charging (UCT03)
  • Energy storage and grid balancing (UCT04)
  • Methanation (UCT05)

Technical view on CCP

CCP: Potential hazardous initial events

  • Loss of electrical supply for particular subsystems
  • Loss of electrical grid connection for or within the CCP infrastructure
  • Loss of communication connectivity
  • ICT Failure of central control facilities (rEMS)
  • Failure of central storage (central battery)
  • Explosion/Fire in the hydrogen plant (H2 Storage, Electrolyser, Compressors)

Identification of spatial dependencies

  • Is the function directly affected by the initial event due to near physical location?
  • Are there other locations that can be threatened by the event?
  • What consequences are likely?

Identification of functional dependencies

  • Which critical functions are directly affected by the spatial interdependencies?
